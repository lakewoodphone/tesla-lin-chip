# Hardware Design Guidelines for a Dual‑LIN, BLE‑Enabled Tesla Steering‑Wheel Passthrough Module (Rev A, ESP32‑S3‑WROOM‑1U, KiCad)

The proposed Rev A design is a compact, automotive‑oriented PCB that bridges two LIN sub‑buses in a Tesla steering‑wheel environment to an ESP32‑S3 for BLE connectivity and USB service, while observing automotive power integrity and safety practices. It must be manufacturable in low volume (5–10 pcs) and scalable to at least 100 units, using KiCad for all CAD artifacts, and staying strictly within robust, CLI‑friendly workflows rather than browser‑bound tools. The core architecture combines an ESP32‑S3‑WROOM‑1U‑N8R8 module for Wi‑Fi/BLE and native USB, two NXP TJA1021T/20 automotive LIN 2.x transceivers for dual‑LIN connectivity, protected 12 V automotive power input with buck‑regulated 3.3 V rails, a USB‑C service port for programming and provisioning, physical mode and arm switches that enforce default‑off LIN transmit behavior, pogo‑pin test access, and an external U.FL‑connected BLE antenna. This report synthesizes Espressif, NXP and major vendor application guidance on ESP32‑S3 hardware, LIN transceiver use, automotive protection, USB‑C, and DFM for 4‑layer PCBs, and then proposes concrete schematic structures, pin assignments, BOM starting points, and layout practices. Where assumptions are uncertain—for example, exact steering‑wheel LIN topology, required isolation between the two LIN channels, or detailed environmental extremes—these are called out explicitly as design‑time risks to validate before committing to fabrication.

## System Context and High‑Level Architecture

### Functional Role of the Passthrough Board

At a conceptual level, the proposed board sits within a Tesla steering‑wheel or column assembly and interfaces to two separate LIN channels. LIN is a single‑wire, low‑speed, cost‑optimized serial bus used heavily for body electronics such as steering‑wheel controls, switches, and actuators, typically at 12 V with bit rates up to 20 kbit/s over up to 40 m of harness.[10] In a typical LIN cluster, one master node and up to fifteen slaves form a deterministic, time‑scheduled network where the master emits a header and individual slaves respond with up to 8 bytes of data.[10][10] Tesla vehicles employ multiple LIN spurs for functions like stalk switches, steering‑wheel buttons, and lighting; the exact topology varies by model and year and must be reverse‑engineered case‑by‑case.[25] The board is intended as a passthrough rather than a gateway that terminates the bus: it should be able to observe, selectively replicate, and optionally re‑transmit LIN frames between upstream and downstream sub‑nodes, while also streaming selected traffic or control state over BLE. This requirement places tight constraints on default safety behavior: the device must be non‑intrusive when unpowered or unconfigured, and the default state must avoid driving the LIN bus into a dominant or otherwise invalid state.[5][5][5][5]

The LIN physical layer is implemented via NXP’s TJA1021T/20 transceivers, which are LIN 2.x and SAE J2602 compliant devices designed for 12 V automotive operation, with integrated LIN termination for responder applications, low electromagnetic emission and high immunity.[5][5][5][5] Each LIN transceiver interfaces to the ESP32‑S3 through 3.3 V CSU‑compatible TXD, RXD, SLP_N and WAKE_N lines; the TJA1021 devices are explicitly 3.3 V / 5 V logic compatible on the digital pins.[5][5][5] This allows direct connection to ESP32‑S3 GPIOs without level shifting.

The central controller is an ESP32‑S3‑WROOM‑1U module, integrating an ESP32‑S3 SoC, 8 MB flash and 8 MB PSRAM (for the N8R8 variant), and a U.FL connector for an external antenna.[1][4] The ESP32‑S3 provides dual‑core Xtensa LX7 processing, rich GPIO, integrated full‑speed USB 1.1 device, and low‑power modes that are especially relevant in an automotive environment where parasitic loads must be controlled.[1][2][19] Espressif’s hardware guidelines for ESP32‑S3 note that the device is powered from a 3.3 V digital rail with a recommended supply current of at least 500 mA to cover peak Wi‑Fi/BLE transmissions and processing bursts.[12][16] This design must therefore provide a robust 3.3 V supply from the 12 V automotive rail, accommodating cold crank, load dump, and transient events.

The power front‑end is thus a critical subsystem. Automotive 12 V systems are nominally around 13.5–14.4 V during operation, but can experience cold crank lows near 5–6 V and load dumps up to 40–60 V.[30] A robust design typically employs reverse polarity protection, a fuse or resettable circuit breaker, TVS diodes for transient absorption, and a buck converter rated for automotive input ranges and current demands.[13][13][24][30] The ESP32‑S3 and LIN transceivers together may draw on the order of 200–300 mA at 3.3 V in peak combined activity, though careful firmware and duty‑cycling can reduce average consumption. Still, Rev A should be designed conservatively, targeting at least 500 mA continuous 3.3 V capability for margin, aligning with Espressif’s general recommendations.[16] NXP’s TJA1021 draws modest quiescent currents but must survive transients on its VBAT pin and provide proper LIN biasing.[5][5][5]

USB‑C service connectivity leverages the ESP32‑S3’s internal USB device PHY, which maps D+ and D− to GPIO20 and GPIO19 respectively.[2][19] Espressif’s USB Device Stack documentation for ESP32‑S3 explicitly notes that these signals should be wired via a USB connector (Micro‑B, USB‑C, or direct plug) with proper impedance control and ESD protection.[2][21] The USB‑C receptacle must be wired as a pure USB 2.0 device, employing pull‑down resistors on CC1/CC2 to advertise as an upstream‑facing device, with no high‑current power delivery negotiation.[33][37] For Rev A, 5 V from USB should be used only for powering the board when disconnected from the vehicle, or at minimum for VBUS detection, and must not backfeed into the vehicle supply.

In addition to logical functionality, the project’s constraints emphasize robust, maintainable KiCad design files, with an eye towards JLCPCB or similar assembly services.[9][17][36] That implies careful library management for footprints and symbols, 4‑layer stackup choices that simplify routing and SI, and DFM features like test pads, fiducials, panelization strategy, and clear silkscreen labeling.[9][14][28][36] A pogo‑pin test fixture, as commonly used in bed‑of‑nails in‑circuit testing, is recommended for efficient programming and bring‑up.[28][38]

The following sections delve into each required topic in more detail, starting with the ESP32‑S3 module hardware design, then the LIN transceiver circuits, automotive power input, TX gating architecture, USB‑C implementation, and ultimately PCBA/DFM practices. The report concludes with a concrete block schematic description, pin assignment table, initial BOM suggestions, and a candid list of unresolved risks to address before ordering Rev A.

## ESP32‑S3‑WROOM‑1U Hardware Design for Rev A

### Module Overview, Powering, and Digital Rails

The ESP32‑S3‑WROOM‑1/1U modules are pre‑certified RF modules embedding Espressif’s ESP32‑S3 SoC, flash memory, and optionally PSRAM.[1] The WROOM‑1U variant replaces the PCB antenna of WROOM‑1 with a U.FL RF connector, enabling flexible external antenna placement, which is valuable in metallic or RF‑noisy environments such as a steering column shrouded by structural metal and airbags.[1][4] The module is powered via a 3.3 V supply; specifically, the ESP32‑S3 SoC has separate VDD3P3_CPU and VDD3P3_RTC pins that must be supplied within 3.0–3.6 V, with 3.3 V as the nominal operating point.[12][16] Espressif recommends that the power supply for ESP32‑S3 deliver at least 500 mA to accommodate peak current drawn during Wi‑Fi/BLE operations, including inrush to decoupling capacitors and simultaneous radio and CPU peaks.[16]

The ESP32‑S3 hardware design guidelines stress the importance of local decoupling capacitors at each power pin grouping.[16] At the digital power pins, a 0.1 µF capacitor placed close to VDD3P3_CPU and VDD3P3_RTC is recommended.[16] The VDD_SPI pin, which supplies external devices like flash or PSRAM, also benefits from 0.1 µF and 1 µF decoupling near the pin; the guidelines caution against excessively large capacitance on VDD_SPI, as that may increase inrush currents and impact the internal LDO startup.[16][32] For module‑based designs, many of these decouplers are already integrated into the module PCB; however, the baseboard must still provide a bulk reservoir capacitor on the 3.3 V rail and nominal local 100 nF decouplers near each major consumer, including the LIN transceivers and any IO expansion.

General supply recommendations from Espressif suggest that when a single 3.3 V supply powers the SoC and peripherals, the regulator’s output current capacity should be at least 500 mA.[16] A 10 µF or larger bulk capacitor at the power entrance, accompanied by an ESD protection diode, is also advised.[16] The latter suggestion aligns with automotive TVS usage where a unidirectional or bidirectional transient suppressor clamps surges before the buck regulator input.[13][13]

From a layout perspective, the module should be placed so that its ground pads connect to a solid ground plane, with short return paths and adequate thermal relief. A 4‑layer stack with the second layer as a continuous ground plane and the third as a power plane is a common, effective topology.[9] This arrangement simplifies RF performance and reduces EMI by providing a uniform reference for both digital and RF currents.

### Boot Strapping Pins, EN Reset, and Auto‑Programming

ESP32‑S3 boot behavior is controlled by several strapping pins, most notably GPIO0 and GPIO46 in conjunction with the EN (CHIP_PU) reset pin.[3][3] Espressif’s esptool documentation explains that the chip will enter the ROM serial bootloader when GPIO0 is held low during reset; otherwise, if GPIO0 is high, the chip will boot from flash and run the application.[3][3] GPIO0 has an internal pull‑up resistor (around 45 kΩ), so leaving it unconnected will cause it to float high and default to normal execution.[3][3] For manual bootloader entry, many development boards provide a “BOOT” or “FLASH” button tied to GPIO0 that pulls it low when pressed.[3][3] ESP32‑S3 also requires that GPIO46 be left floating or driven low in order to enter serial bootloader mode; in normal boot, GPIO46 is ignored, but pulling GPIO46 high while GPIO0 is low can select other boot modes.[3][3]

For automated flashing via a USB‑to‑UART bridge, the esptool documentation instructs designers to connect the converter’s DTR and RTS control lines to GPIO0 and EN respectively.[3][3] By toggling these lines, esptool can assert reset and boot mode sequences without manual button presses. The recommended mapping is EN to RTS and GPIO0 to DTR.[3][3] A modest RC network on EN, typically a 10 kΩ pull‑up to 3.3 V and a 1 µF capacitor to ground, is recommended to filter noise and make automatic reset more reliable.[3][3][39] The presence of an internal 45 kΩ pull‑down/pull‑up network means that external pull‑ups and pull‑downs must be “strong” enough to override strapping during reset; Espressif suggests using around 10 kΩ for strapping resistors connected to GPIO0.[3][3]

In the present design, the native USB device interface of ESP32‑S3 can largely supplant the need for a USB‑UART bridge for programming, because the ROM bootloader can operate over USB as well.[2] However, depending on toolchain stability and provisioning workflows, maintaining a set of UART TX/RX pins on accessible pads is a useful hedge. Espressif’s GPIO matrix allows routing UART signals to almost any GPIO pin.[19] For example, UART0 TX and RX, traditionally on GPIO43 and GPIO44, can be reassigned if those pins are used for other functions, though some forum posts note that using GPIO43/44 with alternate UARTs may be tricky and require careful mux configuration.[22] For Rev A, a conservative approach is to keep one UART instance dedicated to debug, with TX and RX broken out to test pads alongside EN and GPIO0 for emergency recovery.

The physical design should therefore include at least one tiny momentary “BOOT” button connected from GPIO0 to ground, a reset button connected from EN to ground, appropriate pull‑ups and RC on EN, and either a USB‑UART header or test pads for DTR/RTS if automatic serial programming is desired in addition to USB.[3][3][39] Because space is constrained, these buttons may be small side‑actuated or recessed types, but must be accessible for bench development.

### USB D+/D− Routing and Native USB Device Implementation

ESP32‑S3 exposes a full‑speed (12 Mbps) USB 1.1 PHY with its D+ and D− signals internally routed to GPIO20 and GPIO19.[2][19] The ESP‑IDF USB Device Stack documentation states explicitly that for USB device functionality, these GPIOs should be connected to the USB bus via an appropriate connector such as USB Micro‑B or USB‑C.[2] Because the board will use a USB‑C receptacle, the design must map D+ to both D+ pins on the connector, and D− to both D− pins, following USB‑C wiring for USB 2.0‑only devices.[33][37] Care should be taken to maintain 90 Ω differential impedance on the D+/D− pair and to minimize stubs and via transitions, although at 12 Mbps the SI constraints are less stringent than for high‑speed USB 3.x.[21][33]

Protection of the USB data lines against ESD is critical in automotive and service environments where cables may be repeatedly plugged and unplugged under harsh conditions. Semtech and other vendors recommend low‑capacitance TVS diode arrays, such as RClamp4021ZA, specifically designed for USB 2.0 D+/D− lines.[21] These devices provide sub‑1 pF line capacitance, common‑mode or differential protection, and clamping behavior that prevents IEC 61000‑4‑2 ESD surges from reaching the PHY.[21][35] TVS protection should be placed as close as practical to the connector, with short traces to the D+/D− lines and a solid ground reference.[21] For VBUS, which will be nominally 5 V, a separate TVS with appropriate working voltage and clamping level should be used if USB power is allowed to energize the board.

The ESP32‑S3 design must also respect the VBUS detection and powering requirements. When using ESP32‑S3’s native USB device, the chip can detect VBUS presence and connect/disconnect the PHY appropriately.[2] However, because the primary power rail is the vehicle 12 V, the board should avoid back‑feeding 5 V from the vehicle into the USB connector or vice versa. A diode‑OR or ideal‑diode power mux arrangement is common if the board may be powered either from USB or from the vehicle; otherwise, the design can power only logic and ignore 5 V for anything except detection. In either case, the 3.3 V rail should be derived from whichever source is active, while ensuring that no ground loops or hazardous potentials occur.

### Decoupling, Grounding, and Layout for ESP32‑S3

The ESP32‑S3 hardware design guidelines emphasize the importance of well‑planned decoupling and ground layout.[16] Each power pin group requires its own 0.1 µF capacitor placed as close as possible, with short, wide traces to the pins and ground plane. Larger bulk capacitors, such as 10 µF or 22 µF, can be placed slightly farther away but still adjacent to the module to provide local energy storage.[16][32] A 4‑layer stack with a dedicated ground plane directly under the top signal layer is highly recommended; this allows high‑frequency currents to return via low inductance paths and improves RF performance.[9][16]

As with all switching supplies, the buck regulator driving the 3.3 V rail must be laid out to minimize the “hot loop” area between the input capacitor, high‑side switch, diode or synchronous FET, and output capacitor.[40] The guide on buck converter design underscores that high di/dt loops must be compact to reduce EMI, and analog and power grounds should connect at a single point, typically the regulator’s thermal pad.[40] The ESP32‑S3 and LIN transceivers should be referenced to this ground, and care must be taken to avoid splitting the ground plane unnecessarily, especially near the RF region.

Because the module includes a U.FL antenna connector, its RF ground reference is particularly sensitive. Espressif’s RF design guidance (summarized in their hardware guidelines) recommends a continuous ground under the RF section, with no high‑current digital traces routed nearby, and a keep‑out area around the module area corresponding to the PCB antenna region for WROOM‑1; for WROOM‑1U, the module’s keep‑out is mostly mechanical, but the external antenna cable routing and antenna placement must respect manufacturer’s guidelines.[1][16]

### External U.FL BLE Antenna Considerations

The use of ESP32‑S3‑WROOM‑1U implies that the on‑board PCB antenna is replaced by an RF connector for an external antenna.[1][4] The module’s datasheet stipulates that the external antenna must present a 50 Ω impedance at the Wi‑Fi/BLE bands and that the U.FL connector on the module is matched accordingly.[1] The baseboard design must therefore ensure that the U.FL receptacle on the module is unobstructed, that the coax cable can be routed without sharp bends or compression, and that the external antenna can be mounted with a proper ground reference and spacing from metal or carbon fiber structures.

In a steering‑wheel environment, the presence of metal supports, airbags, and wiring harnesses can significantly detune an antenna if placed too close. A practical approach is to route the antenna coax to a small area of plastic trim or a dedicated clip‑on antenna module mounted outside dense metal structures. The external antenna should be an automotive‑rated 2.4 GHz unit with an appropriate connector or adapter, though for Rev A prototyping a standard 2.4 GHz U.FL whip or PCB antenna may suffice. The U.FL connector must be accessible enough to allow replacement during development.

### MSL and Reflow Considerations for the Module

Most ESP32‑S3 modules, like similar RF modules and BGAs, have defined Moisture Sensitivity Level (MSL) ratings that dictate how long they may remain on the manufacturing floor after drypack opening before reflow.[29] TI’s general guidance on MSL explains that components with MSL2 or higher ratings have finite floor lives at 30 °C and 60–85% relative humidity, ranging from one year (MSL2 at 60% RH) down to 24 hours (MSL5a) or “bake before use” (MSL6).[29] Many RF modules are rated around MSL3, meaning a 168‑hour floor life at 30 °C and 60% RH and requiring dry bake if that window is exceeded.[29]

For small prototype runs assembled at JLCPCB or PCBWay, the assembler will manage MSL and reflow profiles, but if in‑house reflow is considered, care must be taken to adhere to the module vendor’s recommended reflow temperature, usually around 245–250 °C peak and limited to one or two reflows.[29] The paste stencil design must follow the module footprint recommendations, typically with segmented ground pads to limit voiding and paste volume, and the keep‑out area beneath RF portions of the module must not contain copper, as specified by the module datasheet.[1][16] It is important to generate accurate KiCad footprints following Espressif’s recommended land patterns, with correct paste and solder mask expansions, and to ensure that design rules for minimum solder mask clearance and pad spacing are compatible with the assembly service’s capabilities.[31]

## TJA1021T/20 LIN Transceiver Circuits and Integration

### LIN Physical Layer and Role of TJA1021

LIN (Local Interconnect Network) is defined as a low‑cost single‑wire automotive serial bus that operates at bit rates up to 20 kbit/s and at voltage levels based on a 12 V or 24 V VBAT reference.[10][10] The physical layer is derived from ISO 9141 (K‑line), with bus levels switching between a recessive high state at VBAT (around 12 V) and a dominant low state near 0 V.[10][10][25] Receivers interpret bus levels above approximately 60% of VBAT as logic 1 and below 40% as logic 0, while transmitters drive more stringent thresholds around 80% and 20% to provide margin.[10] Each LIN node contains a microcontroller, a serial interface (SCI/UART), and a LIN transceiver that translates logic‑level TXD/RXD to the single‑wire bus, and typically includes sleep and wake‑up functionality and integrated pull‑ups.[10][10]

The NXP TJA1021 is a widely used LIN 2.x/SAE J2602 transceiver designed for 12 V automotive environments.[5][5][5][5] It provides physical layer conversion between the microcontroller’s TXD/RXD and the LIN line, including slew rate control to minimize electromagnetic emission and integrated termination resistor for LIN responder applications.[5][5][5] It supports baud rates from 1 kBd to 20 kBd in the /20 and B variants, with high ESD robustness (±6 kV IEC 61000‑4‑2 on LIN, VBAT and WAKE_N), protection against ISO 7637 automotive transients, and thermal shutdown.[5][5][5] The device operates in several modes: Normal, Standby, Sleep, Power‑on, and Wake‑up, with mode transitions controlled via the SLP_N pin and triggered by local or remote wake‑up events on WAKE_N or the LIN bus.[5][5][5][5][5]

For a LIN responder (slave) node, the integrated termination resistor between LIN and VBAT biases the bus high; in a LIN commander (master) node, an additional external resistor and diode network must be added between LIN and VBAT (or INH) to provide stronger pull‑up and biasing.[5][5][5][10][5] In normal mode, the TJA1021’s transmitter drives LIN dominant by pulling it towards ground through the output transistor, and recessive by releasing it so that the termination resistor pulls it up to VBAT.[5][5][5]

### Pin Functions, Power Supply, and Logic Levels

The TJA1021T/20 in SO8 package presents a set of pins that map neatly to microcontroller IO and vehicle supply.[5][5][5][5][5] While the exact pin numbering should always be confirmed from the NXP datasheet, the functional pins include TXD (transmit data input from MCU), RXD (receive data output to MCU), SLP_N (sleep control input, active low), WAKE_N (local wake‑up input, active low), LIN (bus line), VBAT (battery supply), GND, and INH (inhibit output to control external regulator).[5][5][5][5][5] 

VBAT connects directly to the 12 V automotive supply rail, with the device designed to withstand transients and load dump as per ISO 7637; nonetheless, external transient protection via TVS diodes is standard practice.[5][5][5][30] The LIN pin connects to the LIN bus line and includes internal termination to VBAT in responder mode and additional behavior in master configuration.[5][5][5][10] The TXD pin accepts logic‑level input compatible with 3.3 V or 5 V microcontrollers, and RXD outputs corresponding levels, with the datasheet explicitly stating that input levels are compatible with 3.3 V and 5 V devices.[5][5][5] This compatibility eliminates the need for level shifters when interfacing to the 3.3 V ESP32‑S3.

The TJA1021’s SLP_N pin controls mode transitions. A high level on SLP_N transitions the device to Normal mode (after tgotonorm delay), enabling transmit and receive functions.[5][5][5][5][5] A low level on SLP_N initiates a transition to Sleep mode, provided it is held low for at least tgotosleep.[5][5][5][5][5] Sleep mode is the lowest power state, disabling the internal termination between LIN and VBAT and leaving only a weak pull‑up to minimize quiescent current even if LIN is shorted to ground.[5][5][5][5][5] Standby mode is automatically entered upon any wake‑up event while in Sleep, and is indicated by a low level on RXD; INH is driven high and the 30 kΩ termination resistor at LIN is enabled, allowing the external voltage regulator and microcontroller to be activated.[5][5][5][5][5]

Fail‑safe features include pull‑downs on TXD and SLP_N to GND so that if these pins are left unsupplied or floating, the device is forced into a predefined safe state: TXD low and SLP_N low drive the transceiver towards Sleep mode and avoid unintended transmission.[5][5][5][5][5] RXD becomes high‑impedance if VBAT is lost.[5][5][5] The device’s output driver on LIN is protected against overheating and short circuits to VBAT or GND, and there are no reverse currents from the bus if VBAT and GND are lost; the transceiver can be physically disconnected without disturbing the LIN bus.[5][5][5][5][5]

### Mode, Sleep, and Wake‑Up Behavior

Understanding and exploiting the TJA1021’s mode behavior is central to implementing safe default‑off designs. In Power‑on mode, after VBAT is applied, the device initializes and then typically transitions to Sleep mode.[5][5][5] Sleep mode is entered when SLP_N is driven low from Normal mode and held low for tgotosleep, which ensures that the internal termination is disabled and INH becomes floating.[5][5][5][5][5] Sleep is the most power‑saving mode, with only minimal circuitry active; the LIN bus appears as a weak pull‑up to VBAT.[5][5][5][5][5] Despite the low current, the device can still be woken up remotely by a dominant bus state lasting at least twake(dom)LIN, or locally via a negative edge on WAKE_N, or by directly driving SLP_N high.[5][5][5][5][5]

When Sleep is exited due to any wake‑up event, the device first enters Standby mode.[5][5][5][5][5] In Standby, RXD is driven low to signal the microcontroller that a wake‑up occurred, INH goes high to enable an external regulator, and the 30 kΩ termination resistor on LIN is re‑enabled.[5][5][5][5] The microcontroller can then raise SLP_N to enter Normal mode. Normal mode is the functional state where TXD and RXD are active, the transmitter and receiver operate, and the LIN line is driven and monitored.[5][5][5][5][5] In this mode, RXD reports recessive and dominant bus levels as high and low logic respectively, and TXD commands recessive or dominant output.[5][5][5][5][5]

A subtle but important behavior is that in some variants, “as long as TXD is low, the transmitter is off.”[5][5][5][5] This feature allows a short‑circuit on TXD to ground to disable the transmitter and prevent the bus from being stuck dominant. Additionally, a TXD dominant time‑out function exists in related devices such as TJA1029, where a prolonged low on TXD beyond tto(dom)TXD disables the transmitter and releases the bus to recessive.[20] These features serve as a protection against software or hardware faults that would otherwise force a permanent dominant level and block network communication.

### LIN Bus Protection, Termination, and Master/Slave Configuration

On the LIN bus itself, the TJA1021 provides integrated termination appropriate for responder nodes. The LIN output pin is pulled high to VBAT via an internal termination resistor, typically around 30 kΩ.[5][5][5][5][10][5] For a LIN cluster, each slave node has a similar weak pull‑up, while the master node additionally implements a stronger pull‑up, roughly 1 kΩ, between LIN and VBAT through a diode.[10][10] The diode is used so that if a slave loses power, it is not back‑powered through the LIN line; this is in keeping with the guidance that inside each LIN node, LIN and VBAT are connected via a pull‑up resistor and diode.[10][10] The stronger master pull‑up ensures the bus remains firmly recessive when no node is driving, while the weaker slave pull‑ups limit current draw and avoid contention.[10][10]

The TJA1021 can be used in either a responder or commander (master) configuration. For master nodes, the datasheet instructs adding an external resistor in series with a diode between INH or VBAT and LIN.[5][5][5][5][5] This network provides the stronger pull‑up required by the LIN specification. In our case, the Tesla steering‑wheel LIN bus is already mastered by the vehicle’s existing ECU; the passthrough device should behave as a slave or a bridging node rather than introducing a new master. This suggests that the TJA1021 transceivers should be configured as responder nodes, with only their internal termination active and no additional strong pull‑ups. This assumption must be validated in the context of the Tesla harness: if the board is inserted in‑line and segments the bus, the upstream side may need to maintain master‑like termination while the downstream side acts as a slave cluster. However, changing the bus topology from Tesla’s design may have unintended consequences and must be approached carefully.

Regardless of master or slave configuration, LIN bus protection is essential. The TJA1021 already includes high ESD robustness and ISO 7637 transient protection on LIN and VBAT,[5][5][5][5] but external TVS diodes are often added at the ECU connector to clamp fast transients and load dump surges before they stress the transceivers.[13][13][30] Littelfuse application notes describe the use of silicon avalanche diodes (SAD) or TVS arrays for CAN and LIN protection, placing low‑capacitance clamps between LIN and ground and between VBAT and ground.[13][13] These devices handle up to tens of joules of surge energy and protect sensitive electronics without distorting the relatively slow LIN signals. Care must be taken to choose TVS devices with breakdown voltages above the nominal VBAT but below the maximum tolerable voltage of the transceiver and other components.[13][13]

### Pull‑ups, Pull‑downs, and Default Safe States

The TJA1021’s fail‑safe design provides a baseline for safe default behavior. Pin TXD has an internal pull‑down to ground that forces a predefined level if the microcontroller pin is unsupplied, preventing spurious transmissions.[5][5][5][5][5] Pin SLP_N similarly has a pull‑down to ground so that if left floating, the transceiver enters Sleep mode.[5][5][5][5][5] This combination means that if the ESP32‑S3 is unpowered or in reset and its GPIOs are high‑impedance, the TJA1021 will default to Sleep mode with transmitter disabled, and the LIN bus will see only a weak pull‑up.[5][5][5][5][5] This is exactly the sort of default‑off behavior desired in an automotive passthrough device.

However, because the ESP32‑S3’s IO matrix allows flexible assignment, and some pins are strap pins at boot, the hardware designer must ensure that whichever GPIO is chosen to control SLP_N is configured appropriately at reset and early boot to avoid inadvertently waking the transceiver or driving TXD. Pull‑up or pull‑down resistors on the MCU side may need to be aligned with the TJA1021’s internal pulls to guarantee deterministic states.

In Rev A, the combination of TJA1021’s internal pull‑downs and external gating logic (discussed later) can enforce a system‑level “armed” state for LIN transmit. By default, SLP_N can be held low via a resistor and the hardware “arm” gate open; only when the user physically arms the device and the MCU asserts a GPIO high will SLP_N transition to Normal mode and allow LIN transmissions. This layered approach reduces the risk of firmware bugs causing unintended bus interference.

## Automotive 12 V Input and Power Subsystem

### Characteristics of Automotive 12 V Systems

Automotive 12 V systems are notoriously harsh from a power electronics standpoint. While nominal operation occurs around 12–14.4 V with the alternator running, the system can experience a wide range of voltage events. During cold crank, when the starter motor is engaged, the battery voltage can sag to 6 V or lower, yet important ECUs must remain operational.[30] Conversely, during load dump events, where a heavily charged battery is disconnected while the alternator is supplying current, the voltage can spike to 40–60 V for tens to hundreds of milliseconds.[30] Additionally, ISO 7637 describes a suite of transient pulses on the supply lines, including fast spikes from inductive load switching and negative spikes due to inductive kickback.[13][13][30]

Designing electronics to survive these conditions typically involves a combination of protection components: series fuses or circuit breakers to limit sustained overcurrent, reverse polarity protection so that mis‑wiring does not instantly destroy the board, and transient suppression via TVS diodes and input filters.[13][13][30] Littelfuse’s application notes outline the use of TVS diodes as secondary transient protection for sensitive electronics, describing their ability to clamp load dump and other surges to safe levels.[13][13] These TVS diodes are placed as close as possible to the power connector, between VBAT and ground, often paired with input capacitors and series elements that limit di/dt and distribute surge energy.[13][13][30]

### Reverse Polarity Protection Strategies

Reverse polarity protection ensures that if the battery leads are accidentally reversed, the device either remains off or sacrifices an inexpensive sacrificial component instead of the main electronics. Common strategies include series diodes, MOSFET‑based ideal diodes, and fuses combined with shunt diodes.[8] A series diode, either silicon or Schottky, conducts when the polarity is correct and blocks when reversed. Its key parameters are forward voltage drop, which reduces available voltage to the rest of the circuit, and reverse breakdown voltage, which must exceed the worst‑case reverse battery level.[8] As explained in a detailed video and transcript on reverse polarity protection, a series diode must be sized for the forward current and have minimal forward drop if efficiency matters; for low‑current devices under 1 A, a series diode may suffice if the source voltage is not marginal.[8]

However, because the board requires a buck regulator that can accept down to perhaps 6–7 V under crank, the additional drop of even a Schottky diode may unduly reduce headroom. P‑channel MOSFET‑based reverse polarity protection is often preferred, as it introduces only a small RDS(on) and correspondingly low voltage drop while still blocking reverse connection.[8] In such a circuit, the MOSFET body diode orientation and gate biasing are arranged so that in correct polarity, the MOSFET turns on and conducts with low resistance, and in reverse polarity, the body diode is reverse‑biased and the MOSFET is off, preventing conduction.[8] This approach offers near‑ideal behavior and is widely used in automotive and industrial designs.

A third approach uses a shunt diode (across the supply rails) and a series fuse. If the polarity is reversed, the diode conducts heavily and blows the fuse, disconnecting the board from the supply.[8][23] While simple, this is destructive and inappropriate in contexts where blowing fuses in a vehicle harness is unacceptable. For a small module powered from a tap into the steering‑wheel harness, it is better to avoid any configuration that might draw large fault currents capable of affecting other systems. Therefore, a P‑channel MOSFET or ideal diode controller is the recommended strategy for Rev A.

### Fuse or PTC and Circuit Breaker Considerations

Even with reverse polarity protection, it is prudent to include some form of overcurrent protection. Automotive circuit protection options include inline fuses, replaceable blade fuses, resettable PTCs, and automotive circuit breakers.[23] For low‑current modules, a small inline blade fuse or microfuse rated appropriately for the expected current plus margin can be used. Resettable PTC fuses are attractive in that they trip under sustained overcurrent and reset when the fault is removed and the device cools down.[8][23] They are entirely passive and can be integrated on the PCB with no user action, though their resistance and temperature dependence must be accounted for in normal operation.

The choice between PTC and conventional fuses boils down to serviceability and fail‑safe requirements. For prototypes and low‑volume production, PTCs reduce support burden and avoid user intervention, but they may not be strictly “automotive qualified.” Blade fuses are standard in vehicles, but integrating one on a compact steering‑wheel board is awkward; instead, an external harness may include the fuse. For Rev A, a small PTC resettable fuse on the board input offers reasonable protection against internal short circuits without imposing harness modifications.

### TVS and Load Dump Protection

Protecting the board from load dump and other surges requires a TVS diode with appropriate clamping characteristics. Automotive TVS devices are designed to absorb the energy of ISO 7637 pulses and load dump events without failing, and to keep the voltage at or below the maximum ratings of downstream components.[13][13][30] For a 12 V system, devices with standoff voltages around 14–24 V and clamping voltages in the 30–40 V range are common, though exact selection depends on the regulator’s input tolerance and the TJA1021 and other components’ maximum VBAT ratings.[5][5][5][30]

Littelfuse notes that such TVS diodes are ideal for protecting CAN and LIN bus circuits as well as general electronics, and shows typical application circuits with TVS diodes from VBAT to ground and from data lines to ground.[13][13] For the main power input, the TVS should be located immediately after the reverse polarity protection device and before the buck regulator, so that it can clamp surges while the upstream series resistance or MOSFET limits current. A combination of an input electrolytic capacitor (e.g., 47–100 µF) and the TVS can present a reasonably low impedance across the supply, shunting high‑frequency energy.[30][32]

### Buck Regulator Selection for 3.3 V and LIN Supplies

The board requires a 3.3 V rail for the ESP32‑S3 and the digital side of the LIN transceivers, and a VBAT‑referenced supply for the LIN side. The TJA1021 is powered directly from the 12 V VBAT on its VBAT pin, and ESP32‑S3 must not be tied to VBAT.[5][5][5][16] Therefore, the buck regulator’s output is strictly for the 3.3 V domain. Because the ESP32‑S3 may demand up to 500 mA and some margin for future peripherals may be needed, a regulator capable of 1 A continuous output is a prudent choice. The LM5164, for example, is a synchronous buck regulator rated for 6–100 V input and up to 1 A output, optimized for low EMI and rugged applications with –40 °C to +150 °C junction temperature range, and featuring peak and valley current limit, input UVLO, and thermal shutdown.[24] Its wide input range makes it suitable for automotive 12 V systems, including load dump and cranking, provided a suitable TVS is also used.[24][30]

A comprehensive guide on buck converters for hardware design engineers explains that the duty cycle \(D\) of a buck converter in continuous conduction mode (CCM) follows \(D = V_{\text{out}} / V_{\text{in}}\) under steady‑state conditions, ignoring losses.[40] For 3.3 V output from a nominal 12 V input, \(D\) is about 27.5%, dropping to around 5.5% at 60 V during load dump. This wide range underscores the need for a regulator designed for high input voltage and variable duty cycle. Inductor value and output capacitance must be chosen to balance ripple amplitude, transient response, and stability, with larger inductors reducing ripple but slowing response, and low‑ESR output capacitors minimizing voltage ripple.[40] Synchronous converters like LM5164, which replace the traditional diode with a MOSFET, offer higher efficiency, particularly at low output voltages.[40]

The guide further emphasizes layout best practices: tight hot loops, close placement of input and output capacitors near the regulator IC, separation of analog and power ground regions, and a single‑point ground connection at the IC’s thermal pad.[40] Excessive output capacitance can cause high inrush currents at startup, possibly triggering current limits or brownouts, so the total output capacitance must be in line with the regulator’s datasheet recommendations.[32][40] For the ESP32‑S3, starting with around 22 µF of low‑ESR ceramic capacitance near the module and adjusting based on measured transient performance is reasonable.[40][16]

An alternative approach for Rev A is to use an off‑the‑shelf DC‑DC module based on LM2596 or similar, which is widely available and expected in automotive hack projects.[11] However, such modules may not meet EMI constraints or packaging needs, and embedding their circuitry directly into the PCB offers greater control. In any case, the buck regulator must be thoroughly tested under automotive simulation conditions—either in‑vehicle or with a bench supply capable of reproducing crank and surge events—before scaling up production.

### ESD and Transient Protection for LIN and USB

Beyond the primary power input, individual interfaces require localized protection. LIN bus lines, while already somewhat protected by the TJA1021, benefit from additional TVS diodes between LIN and ground, especially at external connectors.[13][13][5] Such TVS devices must be selected for low capacitance to avoid distorting LIN’s 1–20 kbit/s signals and for clamping behavior suitable for 12 V amplitude.[13][13] Similarly, USB D+/D− lines require low‑capacitance ESD protection as described earlier,[21][35] and USB VBUS should be protected by a TVS and, optionally, a resettable fuse if the board may be powered from USB.[21][33]

Automotive circuit protection literature underscores that TVS diodes provide secondary transient protection for sensitive electronics, complementing the first line of defense provided by wiring and central fuse boxes.[13][13][30] In the context of this board, the TVS strategy should consider both the LIN transceivers’ VBAT pins and the main 12 V input to the buck regulator, reusing the same or similar automotive TVS devices for simplicity and cost efficiency.

## Hardware TX Gating and Default‑Off Strategies

### System‑Level Requirements for Safe Default Behavior

Because this board interfaces to a safety‑relevant vehicle network, the most critical non‑negotiable requirement is that it must not unintentionally drive the LIN bus in a way that could disrupt vehicle operation. This requirement holds in several states: when the device is unpowered, when it is powering up or resetting, when the ESP32‑S3 has no valid firmware or is misconfigured, and when the user has not explicitly armed the device for operation. By default, the board should behave as a passive listener or at most a weakly biased node, allowing the existing Tesla ECUs to operate as if the board were absent.

The TJA1021’s internal fail‑safe features already help, by forcing TXD low and SLP_N low when left unsupplied, thereby entering Sleep mode and disabling the transmitter.[5][5][5][5][5] In Sleep mode, the internal termination resistor between LIN and VBAT is disabled and only a weak pull‑up remains, minimizing power dissipation if LIN is shorted and avoiding strong bus biasing.[5][5][5][5][5] This means that if the ESP32‑S3 is off or its GPIOs are high‑impedance, the transceivers will default to a benign state. However, once the ESP32‑S3 is powered, its reset behavior and bootstrapping could drive pins temporarily before firmware initializes, and misconfiguration might inadvertently drive TXD or SLP_N incorrectly.

To address this, a hardware TX gating mechanism should be introduced that ensures that the transceivers cannot leave Sleep mode unless a specific combination of conditions is satisfied, including a physical arm switch and an explicit firmware enable signal. This gating should also allow a physical mode switch to select between passthrough, listen‑only, and perhaps active manipulation modes, though for Rev A it may be sufficient to differentiate between passive listen and active passthrough.

### Practical Gating Mechanisms Using TJA1021 Pins

The most straightforward gating approach leverages the SLP_N pin of each TJA1021. Because SLP_N low forces Sleep and SLP_N high allows entry into Normal mode, controlling SLP_N with a logical AND of the ESP32‑S3’s “armed” signal and a physical switch ensures that the device cannot wake up unless both conditions are met.[5][5][5][5][5] A simple implementation uses a series switch in the SLP_N path and a pull‑down resistor to ground; unless the switch is closed and the MCU drives SLP_N high, the effective level at the pin remains low. For example, one could connect SLP_N to the output of a 2‑input AND gate, where one input is a GPIO from the ESP32‑S3 and the other is the output of a latching arm switch or small analog logic arrangement.

However, to minimize BOM complexity for Rev A, purely passive gating using pull‑ups, pull‑downs, and switches is preferable. A possible scheme is as follows in conceptual terms. SLP_N is pulled down to ground by a resistor (e.g., 100 kΩ) and connected to a SPST slide or rocker switch that, when closed, connects it to a microcontroller GPIO configured with a strong pull‑up or explicit drive high only when the system is armed. When the switch is open, SLP_N is held low regardless of the MCU; when the switch is closed but the MCU has not yet configured the GPIO, the default state of the GPIO should be input with pull‑down, leaving SLP_N low. Only after firmware explicitly configures the GPIO as output high and the switch is closed will SLP_N rise. This approach heavily depends on knowing and controlling the ESP32‑S3’s GPIO default states early in boot.

A safer alternative is to tie SLP_N through a resistor to ground and also via a transistor controlled by the MCU and switch to 3.3 V. For instance, an NPN or N‑channel MOSFET could be used to pull SLP_N high only when both gate/base conditions are met. This external logic adds complexity but reduces the reliance on the MCU’s early boot configuration. Regardless of implementation, the design must ensure that SLP_N cannot float; the transceiver datasheet emphasizes that SLP_N has a pull‑down to force Sleep if unsupplied, but external circuits must not inadvertently fight that behavior.[5][5][5][5][5]

### Default‑Off TXD Considerations

While controlling SLP_N is the primary method for gating transceiver activity, TXD line behavior must still be considered. During Normal mode, TXD dictates whether the transmitter drives LIN dominant or recessive.[5][5][5][5][5] For some TJA1021 variants, as long as TXD is low, the transmitter is off and cannot drive the bus, further contributing to safety.[5][5][5][5] However, the difference between “off” and “recessive” must be carefully understood: in recessive, the termination resistor pulls LIN to VBAT; in off, the transceiver does not actively drive, leaving external terminations to define the bus.

The transceiver includes a fail‑safe pull‑down on TXD, so if the MCU line is floating or unsupplied, TXD will be low and the transmitter disabled.[5][5][5][5][5] To maintain consistent default‑off behavior, the ESP32‑S3’s GPIO used for TXD should be configured as input or output low during and after reset until firmware initializes the LIN stack. Avoiding any default high output or stray toggling during early boot is critical. Because the ESP32‑S3’s strap pins may be multiplexed with GPIOs, care must be taken not to assign TXD to a strapping pin that the boot ROM toggles; using flexible GPIO matrix assignments, TXD and RXD should be placed on non‑strap, non‑USB, non‑flash pins.[19][3]

### Recommended Rev A Hardware Gating Scheme

Balancing complexity, BOM, and safety, a practical Rev A gating recommendation is to rely primarily on SLP_N and the TJA1021’s internal pull‑downs, combined with a single physical arm switch per board that gates SLP_N for both LIN transceivers. This approach is elaborated as follows.

Each TJA1021 SLP_N pin is connected to a common “LIN_EN” net that runs through a SPDT or SPST switch to the ESP32‑S3 GPIO and ground. In the “safe” switch position, SLP_N is hard‑tied to ground via the switch, forcing Sleep regardless of MCU behavior. In the “armed” position, SLP_N is tied to an ESP32‑S3 GPIO (e.g., GPIOx) that defaults to input with pull‑down; an additional 100 kΩ resistor to ground ensures that if the MCU is unpowered or the switch fails open, SLP_N remains low. Firmware, upon initialization, can drive GPIOx high only when the user has confirmed safe conditions (e.g., via BLE command or a second “mode” switch).  

This design ensures that with the physical switch in “safe,” the LIN transceivers cannot exit Sleep. With the switch in “armed,” but the MCU not configured, SLP_N remains low. Only when both the switch is in “armed” and the MCU drives high will the transceivers enter Normal, and even then, TXD remains low by default, preventing active transmission until the LIN stack is fully configured. The physical arm switch thus acts as an interlock for any active bus interaction.

The physical “mode” switch can be connected to separate ESP32‑S3 GPIOs and used purely in software, enabling the firmware to apply different LIN behaviors (e.g., sniff‑only versus active passthrough) while leaving the hardware gating unaffected. Rev A should use a simple 2‑position slide switch for arm gating and a second similar switch or rotary encoder for mode selection.

## USB‑C Service Port Implementation

### Minimal USB 2.0 Wiring with USB‑C

USB‑C is a reversible 24‑pin connector that supports a variety of data and power protocols, but at its simplest, it can be used for USB 2.0 signaling with 5 V power and no USB Power Delivery negotiation.[33][37] The connector has two D+ pins, two D− pins, two CC pins (CC1 and CC2), four VBUS pins, four GND pins, four high‑speed differential pairs for USB 3.x, and two SBU pins for alternate modes.[33][37] For a USB 2.0‑only peripheral such as this board’s ESP32‑S3, the high‑speed pairs and SBU pins remain unconnected, while D+/D−, CC1/CC2, VBUS, and GND are used.[33][37]

The USB Type‑C configuration channel (CC) logic governs cable detection, orientation, and role, and is implemented via pull‑up or pull‑down resistors on CC1/CC2.[33] An upstream‑facing port (UFP) such as a device connects pull‑down resistors (Rd) to ground on CC1 and CC2, typically 5.1 kΩ, while the host or downstream‑facing port (DFP) presents pull‑ups (Rp).[26][33] When a USB‑C cable is connected, only one of CC1 or CC2 will be electrically connected (depending on plug orientation), and the host detects a voltage on that pin, indicating a device connection and allowing current advertisement.[33] For devices that do not implement USB PD, providing 5.1 kΩ resistors from CC1 and CC2 to ground is sufficient to advertise as a default USB 2.0 sink drawing up to 500–900 mA, and most hosts will supply 5 V accordingly.[26][33]

EEVblog forum discussions confirm that the two 5.1 kΩ resistors on CC pins are important when using a USB‑C receptacle as a device port, even if only USB 2.0 signals are used.[26] If only a USB‑C to USB‑A cable is ever used, CC signals may not physically reach the Type‑A side, but that does not relieve the device designer of the need to implement proper CC termination on its receptacle.[26] Therefore, the Rev A board should include two 5.1 kΩ ±5% resistors from CC1 and CC2 to ground, placed near the connector.[26][33]

D+/D− from the ESP32‑S3 must be wired to the D+/D− pairs on the USB‑C connector. The two D+ pins on the receptacle can be shorted together on the PCB and connected to USB_DP (GPIO20), and similarly the D− pins shorted and connected to USB_DM (GPIO19).[2][21][33][37] Care should be taken to maintain good differential pair routing with matched lengths and 90 Ω differential impedance, though at 12 Mbps these constraints are forgiving. GND pins should be tied to the main ground plane, with stitching vias around the connector for mechanical and electrical robustness. VBUS pins, if used for powering or sensing, should be connected together, through a fuse or current‑limiting device, and to a 5 V rail or detection circuit.

### ESD Protection and Power Handling on USB‑C

As discussed earlier, ESD protection on D+/D− is achieved via low‑capacitance TVS arrays placed near the connector.[21][35] Semtech’s RClamp4021ZA, for example, provides ESD protection for two high‑speed data lines and features a minimum 20 V breakdown voltage and low clamping levels, suitable for USB data lines.[21] For VBUS and CC pins, separate TVS devices with appropriate breakdown voltages (e.g., 6–15 V for 5 V rails or 20 V for CC/SBU lines that may see up to 20 V in PD contexts) are recommended.[21] Because this board will not implement USB PD or high voltage power roles, VBUS is expected to remain near 5 V, and CC lines will see only the standard detection voltages.

Protection of CC lines can also employ multipurpose TVS diodes; Semtech notes that CC and SBU lines may need TVS devices with at least 20 V breakdown for PD applications, though in a simple device‑only case, such high ratings may not be strictly necessary.[21] For future‑proofing, using USB‑C protection ICs that integrate ESD protection and line switches for CC may be advantageous, but for Rev A prototypes, discrete 5.1 kΩ resistors and TVS diodes are likely sufficient.

The power handling strategy on USB must also consider coexistence with the 12 V automotive supply. A common arrangement is to allow the board to be powered either from USB or from the vehicle, but not both simultaneously, or to allow both while ensuring that the supplies remain isolated. Using diodes to OR the supplies into a common 5 V or 3.3 V rail is possible, but incurs forward voltage losses and potential ground offset. Alternatively, the design can treat USB as a service‑only port, used for programming and data but not for powering the board in vehicle; in bench settings, the automotive input can be replaced by a 12 V bench supply. For Rev A, a simple configuration would allow powering from either 12 V (primary) or 5 V USB (secondary) using a power mux IC or ideal‑diode arrangement, while isolating the automotive 12 V domain from any backfeed from USB. Regardless, the presence of USB VBUS must not cause any voltage to appear on the vehicle side harness.

### Programming and Recovery Pads

Although native USB provides convenient programming, including a set of pogo‑accessible pads for UART0 TX/RX, EN, and GPIO0 is recommended for recovery scenarios.[3][3][18][28] KiCad allows designers to add test pads easily by placing pad footprints directly on the PCB and assigning them to nets from the schematic; these pads can be used with pogo‑pin fixtures for bed‑of‑nails testers.[18][28][38] A guide on bed‑of‑nails testing explains that a test fixture contains an array of spring‑loaded pogo pins aligned with test points on the PCB, and when the board is pressed onto the fixture, each pin makes contact simultaneously, allowing parallel measurement and programming.[28]

To implement this, the design should include circular or rectangular pads with diameters around 1–2 mm on the bottom side, spaced sufficiently for pogo alignment. These pads should be covered with solder mask openings and not obstructed by components. At minimum, nets for GND, 3.3 V, UART TX, UART RX, EN, GPIO0, and possibly a few GPIOs for programming mode selection or debug should be broken out.

## PCBA, Layout, and DFM Considerations

### KiCad Footprints, Pad Geometry, and Design Rules

KiCad is well suited for CLI‑driven workflows and integrates with version control systems for maintainable design files. Creating or verifying footprints for the ESP32‑S3‑WROOM‑1U and TJA1021 is crucial. The module footprint should follow Espressif’s recommended land pattern, including pad sizes, spacing, solder mask expansion, and paste apertures.[1][16] Pads around the module must respect its mechanical keep‑out and RF no‑copper zones, particularly under any integrated RF circuits.[1] KiCad’s pad dialog lets designers specify drill holes, pad outer diameters, solder mask clearances, and paste percentages; understanding how pad geometry interacts with design rules is key to avoiding DRC errors.[31]

A forum discussion explains that for through‑hole pads, the drilled hole diameter is defined in the footprint, and the pad outer diameter is independent; solder mask openings are calculated as pad OD plus twice the solder mask clearance.[31] Keep‑out areas around pads are determined by the clearance values in the netclass design rules; if another pad or trace encroaches into this area, a DRC error is generated.[31] For SMD components like resistors and capacitors, KiCad’s default footprints may include keep‑out zones that are overly conservative; these can be adjusted via design rules if necessary.[14][27][31]

Design rules such as minimum track width and clearance should be set according to the fabrication house’s capabilities. A KiCad.info discussion suggests that if unsure, setting minimum track width and clearance to around 15 mils (0.35 mm) is a conservative starting point; modern board houses like JLCPCB typically allow much finer features, but prototypes benefit from relaxed rules.[14] For a 4‑layer board with controlled impedance USB traces and fine‑pitch module pads, track widths of 4–6 mils and clearances of 4–6 mils may be appropriate, but JLCPCB’s technical capabilities should be consulted and rules set accordingly.[17][36]

### Four‑Layer Stackup and Routing Strategy

A 4‑layer stack offers comfortable routing, good SI, and clean referencing of high‑speed or RF signals. A widely used stack for IoT boards is: top layer for components and most signals; inner layer 1 as a solid ground plane; inner layer 2 as a power plane or mixed low‑speed routing; and bottom layer for signals, especially those needing to cross others and for test pads.[9] A detailed video walkthrough of a 4‑layer ESP32 IoT PCB design demonstrates using the first inner layer as a power plane distributing 3.3 V and other rails, and the second inner layer as ground, with top and bottom for signal routing.[9] The designer then uses copper zones tied to ground or specific nets on different layers to create low impedance planes and reduce noise.[9]

The same approach suits this board. The buck regulator, ESP32‑S3 module, LIN transceivers, and connectors can all be placed on the top layer to simplify assembly, while the bottom layer hosts test pads and some routing. The ground plane should remain as continuous as possible, with only small voids where necessary for vias and clearances. Power planes or zones for 3.3 V and VBAT on inner layer 1 can simplify distribution and allow thick pours for current handling. Special care must be taken to provide a sufficiently wide VBAT trace and ground returns for the LIN bus and buck regulator.

USB D+/D− should be routed on the top layer over the ground plane, with controlled impedance and minimal via transitions. The ESP32‑S3 module should be placed so that its RF region sees a clear ground reference, and any ground pour around the U.FL connector should be consistent with Espressif’s recommendations.[1][16] The LIN transceivers should be placed near the vehicle connector to minimize LIN trace length and exposure to external noise; their VBAT and LIN pins should be surrounded by ground pours and input protection components (TVS, series resistors if needed).

### Test Pads, Pogo Fixtures, and Fiducials

Designing for test and programming is essential even at low volumes. As noted earlier, bed‑of‑nails fixtures allow rapid in‑circuit testing and programming by bringing pogo pins into contact with designated test pads.[28] JLCPCB and similar manufacturers can provide test fixtures or adapt standard ones if the PCB includes appropriate test pads and fiducials.[28][36] Fiducials are small circular copper markers with solder mask clearance that allow pick‑and‑place machines to align the board; at least two, preferably three, global fiducials should be placed on each panel, and local fiducials can be added near fine‑pitch components such as the ESP32 module.[36]

KiCad makes adding test pads straightforward: designers can create a library footprint for a test point, such as a single plated through‑hole or SMD pad of a given diameter, and then assign it to nets in the schematic. Some forum threads suggest quick hacks such as drawing a filled zone on the mask layer above a via to create a “square” test pad, but for professional designs it is better to use explicit footprints.[18][38] Pogo clip programmers, which grip the edge of the board and contact SMD pads or vias, also require appropriately sized and spaced pads; common pogo clips accommodate around 1 mm diameter holes or pads spaced by a couple of millimeters.[38]

For this board, the bottom layer can include a row of test pads for the core programming and debug signals, and additional pads for key power rails and LIN signals if desired. Clear silkscreen labels near these pads—naming signals such as “TX0,” “RX0,” “EN,” “BOOT,” “GND,” “3V3”—will facilitate manual probing during bench debug.

### Panelization and Assembly Considerations

Even at 5–10 units, designing the PCB so it can be easily panelized pays dividends if a second run of 100 units is planned. JLCPCB’s documentation explains that panelization increases production efficiency, reduces handling time, and can achieve high panel utilization (up to 95%) with V‑scoring or tab‑routing strategies.[36] For a small steering‑wheel module, multiple boards can be arrayed on a single panel with breakaway tabs or V‑scores along the edges. Care must be taken to leave clearance between boards for routing and to avoid placing critical components too close to break lines.

The online JLCPCB interface allows uploading Gerber files, BOM, and component placement lists (CPL) to obtain instant quotes and assembly options.[17] Ensuring that the KiCad project maintains a clean BOM and properly annotated reference designators will reduce friction when moving to assembly. Components such as ESP32‑S3‑WROOM‑1U, TJA1021, buck regulators, TVS diodes, and USB‑C connectors must be confirmed as available in the chosen assembler’s library or provided as customer‑supplied parts.

Silkscreen labels should clearly indicate connector pinouts (e.g., LIN1+, GND, VBAT), switch functions (“ARM,” “MODE”), and the orientation of the board relative to the steering‑wheel harness. Hand‑installed harness parts, such as automotive‑grade connectors or wire pigtails, may not be assembled by JLCPCB and must instead be soldered manually post‑assembly. For these, through‑hole pads with large annular rings and mechanical support features (e.g., holes for cable ties) can increase robustness.

## Recommended Rev A Architecture: Schematic Blocks, Pinout, and BOM

### Block Schematic Description

The Rev A board can be conceptualized as comprising the following functional blocks, which map to the sections discussed above.

At the top level, the board receives a 12 V automotive input from the steering‑wheel harness. This input passes through a reverse polarity protection stage, implemented via a P‑channel MOSFET configured as an ideal diode, followed by an inline resettable PTC fuse sized for the board’s maximum current. Immediately downstream, a high‑power automotive TVS diode clamps VBAT to safe levels during transients and load dump events.[13][13][30] A bulk input capacitor smooths the supply and provides energy for the buck regulator.

The buck regulator (e.g., LM5164 or equivalent) converts the protected VBAT to 3.3 V, with appropriate inductor, input and output capacitors, feedback network, and compensation, designed for continuous conduction at the expected currents and automotive voltage range.[24][40] The 3.3 V rail powers the ESP32‑S3 module, the digital side of the LIN transceivers (where applicable), and miscellaneous logic. The VBAT rail, after TVS protection, powers the TJA1021 VBAT pins and is distributed to the LIN bus connectors.

The ESP32‑S3‑WROOM‑1U module sits at the core, its 3.3 V and ground pins connected to the 3.3 V plane and ground plane with decoupling capacitors as per Espressif’s recommendations.[16] The module’s EN pin is pulled up to 3.3 V via a 10 kΩ resistor and connected to ground via a 1 µF capacitor and a reset pushbutton for manual reset.[3][3][39] GPIO0 is exposed via a BOOT pushbutton to ground and a 10 kΩ pull‑up to 3.3 V, with optional mapping to a USB‑UART DTR line if a UART bridge is included.[3][3] Native USB D+/D− pins (GPIO20/19) route to the USB‑C connector’s D+/D− pins, with ESD TVS arrays protecting them.[2][21][33][37] CC1 and CC2 pins on the USB‑C connector each connect to ground through 5.1 kΩ resistors.[26][33][37]

Two TJA1021T/20 LIN transceivers are placed near the board’s LIN harness connectors. Each has its VBAT pin connected to the protected 12 V rail, its LIN pin connected to the corresponding LIN line through an optional series resistor and TVS diode, its GND pin tied to the main ground plane, and its TXD, RXD, SLP_N, and WAKE_N pins connected to the ESP32‑S3.[5][5][5][5][5] TXD and RXD pins use 3.3 V logic and connect to GPIOs assigned via the ESP32 IO matrix to a UART or bit‑banged LIN interface. SLP_N of both transceivers is tied together and controlled via a hardware arm switch and ESP32 GPIO as described previously, providing a common gating mechanism.[5][5][5][5][5]

A physical mode switch connects to two or more ESP32 GPIOs and allows selection of operation modes (e.g., off, sniff, passthrough). LEDs indicating power, LIN activity, and BLE status may be added and tied to dedicated GPIOs, but should be considered optional for Rev A to save space. The module’s U.FL connector is left accessible, and the external antenna is routed away from metal structures.

Pogo‑pin test pads on the bottom layer provide access to ground, 3.3 V, UART TX, UART RX, EN, GPIO0, and possibly SLP_N and a spare GPIO for test. These pads are sized for pogo pins and indicated on the silkscreen or documentation.

### Unresolved Risks and Assumptions to Validate

Several assumptions in this architecture require validation before committing to production‑level quantities.

First, the Tesla steering‑wheel LIN topology must be characterized. It is assumed that there are two separate LIN channels that can be intercepted without impacting other ECUs, and that the board can behave as a passive node on each without violating the cluster’s master‑slave role distribution.[10][25][10] If Tesla’s wiring uses unusual LIN topologies or proprietary variants, the board may require more complex behavior or isolation.

Second, the thermal environment inside the steering‑wheel assembly is uncertain. Ambient temperatures may reach high values, especially in hot climates with sun exposure, and transient heating from cabin HVAC ducts could occur. The buck regulator, transceivers, and ESP32 module must be derated accordingly, and their junction temperatures kept within safe limits. It may be necessary to use a regulator with enhanced thermal performance or heatsinking features.[24][30][40]

Third, EMI requirements for in‑vehicle electronics can be stringent. While the LM5164 and TJA1021 are designed for low EMI, layout, shielding, and filter design will influence the actual emission levels.[24][5][5][40] The board may require input filters (LC pi filters) and shielding to pass OEM EMC tests. Rev A should be instrumented for EMI characterization, but formal tests may be deferred until after functional validation.

Fourth, the use of native USB as the primary programming interface depends on the robustness of Espressif’s USB bootloader and host drivers. In case of issues, the UART bootloader path must be well supported via test pads; this risk is mitigated by including both programming pathways.[2][3][3]

Finally, the chosen reverse polarity protection and TVS scheme must be validated against realistic fault scenarios, including jump‑starting, battery disconnection, and harness shorts.[13][13][30] Simulations and bench tests with appropriate surge generators or automotive‑grade supplies are recommended before moving beyond Rev A.

## Conclusion

This report has developed a detailed hardware design concept for a Rev A dual‑LIN, BLE‑enabled Tesla steering‑wheel passthrough board built around an ESP32‑S3‑WROOM‑1U module, two NXP TJA1021 LIN transceivers, a protected 12 V automotive input, USB‑C service port, and physical gating mechanisms for safe default‑off behavior. Drawing from Espressif’s hardware design guidelines, NXP’s TJA1021 datasheet, automotive protection application notes, USB‑C and ESD protection literature, and KiCad/DFM best practices, the report has framed concrete recommendations on power supply design, LIN transceiver configuration, USB implementation, TX gating, layout, and testability.[1][2][3][5][5][12][16][5][21][24][5][10][33][36][40]

At the device level, the ESP32‑S3‑WROOM‑1U is powered by a robust 3.3 V rail derived from an automotive‑rated buck regulator such as the LM5164, with appropriate decoupling and careful ground layout to support Wi‑Fi/BLE peaks and minimize EMI.[16][24][40] Bootstrapping pins EN and GPIO0 are wired according to Espressif’s guidance, with RC filtering and pushbuttons for reset and bootloader entry, and optional connections to a UART bridge via DTR/RTS to support automated flashing.[3][3][39] Native USB D+/D− lines connect to a USB‑C receptacle, with CC1/CC2 pull‑downs of 5.1 kΩ to advertise as a device and ESD TVS arrays protecting D+/D−.[2][21][26][33][37]

For LIN connectivity, each TJA1021 transceiver is powered from the protected 12 V VBAT rail, its digital pins interfaced directly to 3.3 V ESP32 GPIOs, and its LIN pin wired to the LIN branch through optional series resistance and TVS protection.[5][5][5][10][5] Internal fail‑safe pull‑downs on TXD and SLP_N ensure that in the absence of MCU control, the transceivers enter Sleep mode with transmitter disabled and only a weak pull‑up biasing the bus.[5][5][5][5][5] A system‑level gating strategy built around SLP_N, controlled jointly by a physical arm switch and an ESP32 GPIO, enforces a conservative default, allowing LIN transceivers to enter Normal mode only when explicitly armed and commanded by firmware.

The automotive 12 V input is conditioned by a reverse polarity protection stage, likely a P‑channel MOSFET‑based ideal diode, followed by a PTC resettable fuse and an automotive TVS diode to clamp load dump and other surges.[8][13][13][23][30] Input capacitors and careful placement of the buck regulator complete the power front‑end. Additional TVS devices on LIN lines and USB further improve robustness.[13][13][21][5]

KiCad‑based PCB design is supported by well‑chosen footprints, a 4‑layer stack with solid ground plane and structured power distribution, and DFM features including fiducials, test pads for pogo‑pin fixtures, and panelization strategies amenable to JLCPCB or similar assemblers.[9][14][17][28][31][36] The report’s tables provide starting pin assignments for the ESP32‑S3 and initial BOM candidates for key components, though final selection should be driven by detailed calculations, availability, and cost.

Several risks remain to be addressed through further research and testing, including Tesla‑specific LIN topology confirmations, thermal and EMI performance in the steering‑wheel environment, and validation of the reverse polarity and TVS protection under real automotive events.[10][25][30][10] Nonetheless, the outlined design principles and components form a solid foundation for a Rev A proof‑of‑concept that balances safety, functionality, and manufacturability, while staying within a KiCad‑centric, CLI‑friendly workflow that supports future iterations and scaling to at least 100 units.
